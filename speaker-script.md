# Speaker Script — Ivoclar Enablement Engine
## Samuel Nord | 30 minutes | 14 slides

Practice this out loud. Read it like you are talking to real people, not reading a document.
Time yourself. You want about 28 minutes — leaves 2 minutes buffer.

---

## SLIDE 0 — Executive Overview (~2 min)

Good afternoon. Thank you for having me back.

Before I go into the details, let me give you the big picture first.

**[pause — look around the room]**

Ivoclar IT today works as a cost center. It takes orders. It delivers. But it does not steer.

My plan is to change that. I want to turn IT into a value engine.

That means three things.

First — transparency. Everyone sees the pipeline. What it costs. What it delivers.

Second — value. We measure outcomes, not just activity.

Third — AI-readiness. Not more pilots. The governance to actually scale them.

And everything backed by real financials — TBM and FinOps — so we prove value in Euros, not in PowerPoint.

Let me walk you through each layer.

---

## SLIDE 1 — The 50/30/20 Portfolio (~2 min)

Right now, IT spending is reactive. Whoever shouts loudest gets resources. I want to change that with a clear split.

Fifty percent goes to strategic value — projects that actually grow the business. CRM, digital workflows, market enablement.

Thirty percent goes to IT health — keeping things safe and modern. And that includes cleaning up unused licenses. Today, licenses are bought and forgotten. That stops.

Twenty percent goes to innovation — AI experiments in a safe sandbox. Structured pilots with clear gates, not open-ended experiments.

**[pause]**

This ratio is not a fixed rule. The Demand Board reviews it every quarter. But having a target stops the drift.

---

## SLIDE 2 — The 4-Pillar Strategy (~2.5 min)

Here is the strategy in simple language. Four pillars. Each one solves a real problem from the case study.

**[point to the table]**

Demand — we stop hallway requests. One Front Door. One way in.

Priority — we stop the loudest voice from winning. We use a scoring system instead. Impact, effort, strategy fit. The Demand Board decides — not politics.

AI — we stop the unsecured hype. We build a sandbox-to-scale funnel. Experiment safely, then prove data readiness and ROI before you scale.

Process — we stop digitizing the mess. Global Process Owners have to approve the workflow before we automate anything. Standardize first, then digitize.

Four problems, four answers.

---

## SLIDE 3 — TBM & FinOps (~2.5 min)

None of this means anything if we cannot show the money.

That is where TBM and FinOps come in. Two views on the same question — where does the money go, and is it worth it?

**[pause]**

The macro view is TBM — Technology Business Management. We take every Euro spent on IT and map it to the business unit it supports. So we can answer things like: what does IT cost for dental product development? Today, IT costs are a black box. TBM opens it.

**[pause]**

The micro view is FinOps. Think of it like a bakery. You do not just know your total costs — you know what it costs to make one croissant.

We apply the same thinking to AI. One AI-written summary costs twelve cents. Doing it by hand costs four fifty. That saves money — scale it.

But one AI quality check costs eight twenty. Doing it by hand costs three. That loses money — stop it.

Simple math.

**[pause]**

Together, TBM and FinOps are the financial cockpit of the Enablement Engine.

---

## SLIDE 4 — Success Scorecard (~2 min)

Four KPIs that tell us if the engine works.

Cost recovery — we find software nobody uses and stop paying for it. Target: save fifteen percent of license costs in year one. That is the fastest win.

Decision speed — today people wait weeks for an answer on their request. Target: cut that waiting time by thirty percent.

AI efficiency — every AI solution must cost less than doing the same job by hand. If not, we do not scale it. Simple rule.

Portfolio balance — fifty percent growth, thirty percent health, twenty percent innovation. Every quarter, we check if the split is still right. If not, we rebalance.

Four numbers. Board-level clarity.

**[pause — transition]**

Now let me show you how we build this. I will use a picture.

---

## SLIDE 5 — The Airport + Pain Points (~2 min)

Imagine Ivoclar IT is a busy international airport.

Planes — your IT requests — landing from everywhere. Pilots calling different numbers. Landing on side runways. Sometimes not even announcing.

There is no single control tower.

**[pause — let it land]**

That is today. IT delivers — but it does not steer.

What is broken? Four things.

First — no flight control. Requests through side channels. No single front door.

Second — the runway is blocked. Too many projects. Loudest voice lands first.

Third — AI hype. Lots of test flights, no rulebook. Nobody knows which are safe to scale.

Fourth — every terminal works differently. Processes fragmented. Wasting time.

These four are not separate. They form a chain.

---

## SLIDE 6 — Pareto (~2 min)

Now — not all four problems are equal. As a project manager, my first instinct is Pareto. Where does the pain actually sit?

**[point to the chart]**

Look at this. Intake and the missing front door — thirty-eight percent. Portfolio prioritization — twenty-seven percent. Together, that is sixty-five percent already.

**[pause]**

The point is simple: if you fix just the first two — intake and prioritization — you solve about eighty percent of the chaos.

The AI governance and process standards? They become much easier once the core engine is running.

So my approach: build the control tower first. Then the flight plan. Then the test programs. Do not try to fix everything at once.

---

## SLIDE 7 — The Engine: Control Tower + Governance (~3 min)

So here is the engine. Component A — the control tower.

**[point to the top row]**

You see three groups at the top. Business partners — the departments who need IT to do something. IT delivery — the people who build and run. And the GPOs — that stands for Global Process Owners. These are the people who own the end-to-end business processes, like order-to-cash or product lifecycle. They define how work should flow before we automate anything.

All three groups submit their requests through the same Front Door. One way in. No side channels.

**[point to the Front Door box]**

Behind the Front Door sits a triage. Small requests get fast-tracked. Bigger ones go down to governance.

**[point to the bottom row]**

Governance is lean. IT Steering Committee quarterly — the big strategic calls. Demand Board bi-weekly — that is where the real prioritization happens, business and IT together. AI Review Board monthly — same logic, but with an extra gate for data readiness.

**[pause]**

Everyone sees the same flight board. No shortcuts.

And here is what makes this different from a regular org chart — every decision these boards make is backed by telemetry. Real data. Usage metrics. Cost per service. Adoption rates. Performance trends.

**[pause — this is important]**

No more gut feeling. No more loudest voice. The data decides. That is how you move from politics to priorities.

---

## SLIDE 8 — Demand-to-Value (~2.5 min)

Component B — the flight plan. Six steps from idea to value.

One — intake through the Front Door.

Two — triage. Small stuff gets fast-tracked.

Three — a lightweight business case. I really mean lightweight. Three questions: What is the expected outcome? How do we measure it? Who owns the benefit? That is it.

Four — the Demand Board decides the priority.

Five — execution with a clear business owner. Not just IT alone.

**[pause]**

And six — this is the one most companies skip — value check after go-live. Did we actually deliver what we said we would?

A flight is not successful because it took off. It is successful because passengers arrived on time.

---

## SLIDE 9 — AI + FinOps Gate (~2 min)

Component C — AI orchestration. Or the test flight program.

The problem is not too few AI ideas. It is no structure to evaluate them.

My approach is four steps.

First — the AI scorecard. Think of it as a simple checklist for every AI idea. Three questions: Is it useful? Is it doable? And — this is the extra one — is the data actually ready? Because the best AI idea is useless if the data is not there. If the answer is not yes to all three, the idea waits.

Second — guardrails. These are the safety rules nobody can skip. Data must be secure. The architecture must be approved. And someone must own it. No exceptions.

Third — sandbox-to-scale. That means: try small before you go big. We give teams a safe space to test their AI idea. If it works, we grow it step by step. Pilot, then MVP, then production. Gates at every step.

Fourth — the FinOps gate. This is the money check before we scale anything. Does this AI save more money than it costs to run? If yes, scale it. If no, stop it. Simple.

**[pause]**

And the key rule: AI competes in the same portfolio as every other IT project. One flight board, one language.

---

## SLIDE 10 — Shadow Demand (~2 min)

One challenge I want to address head-on.

In a global organization, not all demand comes from Schaan. Some comes from Latin America, Asia, other regions — where the local team reports to the regional CEO, not to headquarters IT.

I call these private jets. They land on side runways, outside the system. Someone buys a tool, starts an AI subscription — not to break rules, but because it is faster.

**[pause]**

You cannot stop this by building walls. People bypass IT because the official way is too slow. So we make the official way faster.

Four things.

First — a fast clearance lane. Regional IT partners can approve small, low-risk requests on the spot. No need to wait for headquarters.

Second — turn on the radar. We run a regular check of all subscriptions and software across regions. No blame — just visibility. You need to see what is already flying before it becomes a problem.

Third — procurement integration. Finance flags unknown subscriptions when the bill comes in. Every tool needs an owner.

Fourth — delegated control towers. Each region gets a local enablement lead who can act fast — but within the same global framework.

**[pause]**

The principle: make the tower so good that everyone wants to use it.

---

## SLIDE 11 — 365-Day Plan + Quick Wins (~3 min)

Three hundred and sixty-five days. Three phases.

Phase one, day one to sixty: I listen. Stakeholder interviews. Pain mapping. Understand the portfolio, processes, AI landscape.

And — this is important — I start the TBM cost-mapping. What does that mean? We take the IT budget and connect every line to a business unit or service. So instead of one big number, you can see: this much goes to dental, this much to adhesives, this much to infrastructure. That is the baseline. No changes yet — just building the picture. And building relationships. That comes first.

**[pause]**

Phase two, day sixty-one to one hundred thirty: launch and win.

Quick win one — the Front Door itself. A portal where every request gets acknowledged in forty-eight hours. Not perfect. Working. That is the biggest signal that things are changing.

Quick win two — the portfolio reset. I sit down with leadership and we make one joint call: continue, pause, or stop. Frees capacity right away.

Quick win three — the radar sweep. First inventory of subscriptions and shadow IT. No blame — just visibility.

Also in this phase: the Demand Board starts meeting bi-weekly, and we build the first TBM dashboard — a clear view of where every Euro goes.

**[pause]**

Phase three, day one hundred thirty-one to three hundred sixty-five: stabilize, embed, and grow.

Governance matures. First AI pilots go through proper gates. Process fragmentation goes down.

And here is something I want to add — we build a FinOps practice. That is a small team that tracks unit costs across IT and AI services. What does it cost to run this? Is it worth it? They answer those questions.

**[pause — slight smile]**

And a bonus idea: we gamify the results. Teams that save the most, or deliver the best value-to-cost ratio, get recognized. Make saving money and creating value something people compete on — not something that feels like a punishment.

By day three hundred sixty-five, we deliver the fifty-thirty-twenty portfolio view to the board. The engine is running.

---

## SLIDE 12 — Stakeholders (~1.5 min)

One more thing before I close. The best engine in the world does not work if people do not use it.

For business partners — they are co-designers, not customers. I involve departments from the start. They help shape the system, so they actually want to use it. If people feel heard, they show up.

For the IT team — and I say this deliberately — I do not replace experts. Mike, Simon, their teams — they know the systems better than I ever will. My job is to give them a clear framework and remove blockers. Not to overrule them.

Communication — when something works, everyone should know. Regular updates, no surprises. When the Front Door cuts response time, we share that story. Upward to leadership, sideways to peers.

**[pause]**

And my change philosophy — this is very much who I am. I do not force change on people. I show results first, then they want to join. Small wins build trust faster than big plans. Pull, not push. That is how I have always worked.

**[slight smile — you mean it]**

---

## SLIDE 13 — Closing (~1.5 min)

Let me close where I started.

**[pause — slow down here]**

Ivoclar's IT challenge is not technology. It is not budget. It is a missing operating system.

Unmanaged demand. Unclear decision rights. Inconsistent execution. That creates low transparency and low value perception.

The case study asked me to build the Business and IT Enablement Engine.

That is exactly what I have laid out. An engine that connects demand, decisions, delivery, and value. Governance that works across regions. Financial transparency through TBM and FinOps. A portfolio balanced at fifty-thirty-twenty.

**[pause]**

The airport was busy. The planes were flying. What was missing was the engine that makes it all work together.

I would like to build it.

Thank you.

**[pause — wait for them]**

Happy to take your questions.

## REMINDERS FOR PRACTICE

- **Total time target:** 28 minutes (leaves 2 min buffer before Q&A)
- **Speak slower than you think.** Especially on the closing slide.
- **Pauses are your friend.** They make you sound confident, not nervous.
- **Eye contact** when you say "I would like to build it." Look at Markus Strasser.
- **The croissant analogy** is your secret weapon. It makes FinOps memorable. Lean into it.
- **Don't read the slides.** They are visual anchors. You tell the story.
- **If you lose your place:** go back to the airport metaphor. Everything connects to it.
- **Names matter:** mention Mike, Simon by name on the stakeholder slide. Shows you did your homework.
